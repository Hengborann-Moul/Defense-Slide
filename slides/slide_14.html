METHODOLOGY · FRAME SELECTION

How Frames Are Extracted and Selected Per Clip

Raw DAiSEE video is 30 fps with variable clip duration. We downsample to 15 fps and standardize every clip to 150 frames — about 10 seconds of behavior — to balance temporal resolution against compute cost.

01

slow_motion_video30 fps → 15 fps downsample

Skip every other frame from the original 30 fps recording. Halves compute without losing meaningful expression dynamics (most facial actions live below 5 Hz).

02

view_weekUniform 150-frame window

For clips longer than 150 frames (~10 s at 15 fps), select frames at regular intervals to preserve temporal structure. 150 frames was chosen via ablation (see Slide 28).

03

all_inclusiveLast-frame padding for short clips

For clips shorter than 150 frames, repeat the final frame to maintain sequence length. This preserves the input dimensionality expected by downstream feature engineering.

04

verified_userMissing-detection handling

Clips with zero face detections are excluded. Clips with partial detections must retain at least 150 frames with valid face features — quality control that ensures reliable feature representations.

OUTPUT TENSOR
X ∈ ℝ(N × 150 × 78)

Where N is the number of video clips, T = 150 is the sequence length, and D = 78 is the per-frame feature dimensionality.

Why 150 frames?
  • 30 frames → too short, misses slow boredom drift
  • 150 frames → elbow point (see Slide 28 ablation)
  • 300 frames → plateau, adds transition noise

Frame extraction begins with a deliberate 30-to-15 fps downsample — we skip every other frame. This halves the compute budget without losing meaningful expression dynamics, because most facial actions live below 5 Hz. We then standardize every clip to 150 frames, roughly 10 seconds at 15 fps. For longer clips we sample at uniform intervals to preserve temporal structure; for shorter clips we repeat the final frame. The 150-frame choice is not arbitrary — it is the elbow point in the ablation on slide 28, after which performance plateaus and transition noise creeps in. Finally, quality control drops clips with zero face detections, which removes roughly 5 percent of the corpus.
