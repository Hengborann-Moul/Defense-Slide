The motivation for this thesis is rooted in a concrete Cambodian event. On March sixteenth, twenty twenty, the Ministry of Education ordered the closure of approximately thirteen thousand three hundred schools nationwide in response to the COVID-19 pandemic. Almost overnight, every learner moved from a physical classroom to a laptop screen, and every teacher lost the most basic feedback channel they had always relied on — the ability to read a student's face and sense whether the material was landing. Without that signal, just-in-time pedagogical support collapsed. This thesis attacks exactly that loss: it asks whether we can rebuild the feedback loop computationally by detecting boredom, engagement, confusion, and frustration from a webcam feed, and crucially whether we can do it in near real time — meaning minutes, not hours, after the affective state occurs.
