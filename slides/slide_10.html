The contributions of this thesis span three dimensions. On the theoretical side, the work tests whether deep learning is truly necessary for attention recognition, it identifies the conditions under which a lightweight model matches or beats deep networks, and the S-H-A-P analysis reveals which facial features actually drive each attention state, advancing the theoretical understanding of affective computing. On the methodological side, we contribute a reproducible facial-feature pipeline for DAiSEE that combines geometric, temporal, and statistical features, and we evaluate accuracy, compute cost, and interpretability together rather than separately. On the practical side, the framework cuts computational cost by orders of magnitude relative to deep learning, which enables real-time monitoring on budget laptops and mobile devices, and the interpretable output guides timely and targeted teaching interventions. This practical contribution is what makes the thesis deployable today in Cambodian classrooms. The work also aligns with U-N Sustainable Development Goals four and nine, providing a policy hook for future funding.
